Most write-ups about "small" embedding models stop at a benchmark table: here is the
retrieval score at 7B, here it is at 300M, look how little we lost. That framing hides
where the real work is. On a phone, the score is rarely what breaks first.

## The budget is not the model

A gallery search feature has to share the device with everything else. The constraint
that bites is not "how big is the checkpoint" but a set of numbers that never show up in
a leaderboard:

- **Peak memory during inference**, not the weight file size. A 300M model that spikes
  to 1.2GB while encoding a batch is unusable even though it "fits".
- **Cold start**, because a search box has to feel instant the first time it is opened,
  not the third.
- **Thermal headroom**, since sustained encoding over a photo library is a very
  different load from a one-shot query.

Optimizing for the checkpoint size alone tends to make two of these worse.

## Quantization moves the error somewhere specific

The usual claim is that int8 quantization costs "about a point" of accuracy. Averaged
over a benchmark, sure. But the loss is not spread evenly — it concentrates in the
region of embedding space where vectors are already close together.

That is precisely the region retrieval depends on. Two photos of the same person on the
same day are near-duplicates in embedding space, and those are the pairs that quantization
error flips. The benchmark barely moves because such pairs are rare in the test set and
common in a real photo library.

The practical consequence: evaluate on the tail, not the mean. A histogram of pairwise
distance shifts tells you more than a single retrieval number.

## Evaluation has to mirror the device

The gap I keep returning to is that we evaluate embeddings under conditions the phone
never sees. Benchmarks use clean, deduplicated, balanced corpora. A real gallery is
none of those — it is thousands of near-identical shots, long stretches of one place,
and a handful of genuinely distinct events.

None of this means the benchmarks are useless. It means a benchmark result is a
necessary condition, not a sufficient one, and the interesting engineering starts after
the table looks acceptable.
